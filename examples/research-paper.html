Measuring the hidden coordination cost of neighborhood climate plans

Abstract

Most climate plans describe funding gaps. Fewer documents make the cost of coordination visible while the plan is still changing.

1. Introduction

Local climate adaptation work is often described as an engineering problem with a community engagement layer attached. That framing misses the day-to-day negotiation required to translate a high-level resilience goal into budget lines, permit language, maintenance roles, and public-facing promises.

The result is a review process where the same paragraph is approved by one stakeholder, reopened by another, and rewritten weeks later when the funding source changes. We use coordination cost to describe the extra time, attention, and institutional risk created by those repeated alignment loops.

2. Research questions

The study asks three questions. Which plan components create the most recurring coordination work? Which documents or rituals reduce ambiguity before public review? How can planning teams make coordination cost visible without turning every update into a reporting burden?

These questions are useful for annotation testing because a single sentence can carry a claim, a caveat, and a policy implication. Anchored comments need enough context to remain actionable after the author edits the draft.

3. Data and coding

The initial sample includes twelve neighborhood plans, twenty-four meeting agendas, and eight interview transcripts from planning staff. Each source is coded for decision owner, dependency language, deadline confidence, and evidence of unresolved disagreement.

Code Definition Review prompt Annotation stress
Decision owner The person or group named as responsible for resolving an open choice. Is the owner named clearly enough for follow-up? Dense table text and narrow cells
Deadline confidence Whether the document implies a hard date, flexible milestone, or external dependency. Should this date be softened before public review? Short phrase selection inside rows
Unresolved disagreement Evidence that stakeholders accepted a sentence without accepting the same trade-off. Does this paragraph hide a real disagreement? Cross-reference between table and body copy

4. Preliminary finding

Preliminary coding suggests that coordination cost spikes when a plan shifts from describing problems to assigning responsibility. General statements about heat risk, stormwater stress, or mobility access often move quickly through review. Statements that assign a maintenance role, narrow the target geography, or imply a funding source are more likely to trigger repeated edits.

The largest review burden appears when a sentence is technically accurate but politically underspecified.

This matters because many teams do not track the cost of revisiting an already approved section. The visible artifact is a cleaner paragraph, but the hidden work is a sequence of calls, rewritten slides, new caveats, and side documents that explain the change.
